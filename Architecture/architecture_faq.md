# **Architecture FAQ**
### *Common conceptual questions about the ED‑Primitives architecture*

This FAQ addresses recurring conceptual questions about the **architecture** of ED.  
It does **not** discuss physics, PDEs, experiments, or empirical interpretations.  
It focuses solely on the **structural ontology** defined by the primitives.

---

# **1. Why does ED need 13 primitives?**

Because the architecture requires **exactly enough structural ingredients** to:

- generate contrast  
- stabilize structure  
- propagate commitments  
- support multiplicity  
- produce motifs  
- scale into networks  

Fewer primitives collapse the architecture into uniformity.  
More primitives introduce redundancy without adding structure.

Thirteen is the **minimal complete set** for a geometry‑first ontology.

---

# **2. What is the role of gradients in ED?**

Gradients are the **calculus** of ED — the way structure changes across the architecture.

They are not derivatives or PDE objects.  
They are **directional tendencies** that:

- create contrast  
- enable sign‑flips  
- seed saddles  
- support motif formation  

Gradients are the **engine of variation**.

---

# **3. What is a gradient sign‑flip, really?**

A sign‑flip is a **relational inversion** of directional tendencies.

It is the architectural moment when:

- one direction trends “up”  
- another trends “down”  
- the contrast cannot be resolved uniformly  

This inversion forces the formation of **saddles**, which then produce **boundaries** and **horizons**.

The sign‑flip is the **generator of structure**.

---

# **4. Why is the saddle → boundary → horizon triad so important?**

Because it is the **universal architectural pipeline** produced by primitive interactions.

```
Saddle → Boundary → Horizon
geometry   topology   dynamics
```

This triad is:

- the minimal structure that can exist  
- the seed of all motifs  
- the foundation of all higher architecture  

It is the **structural backbone** of ED.

---

# **5. What is a motif in ED?**

A motif is a **stable, recurring structural pattern** built from repeated triads.

Motifs are:

- the first emergent structures  
- the building blocks of larger networks  
- the patterns that persist under propagation  

Motifs are **architecture before physics**.

# **6. How does structure propagate in ED?**

Structure propagates through:

- motif repetition  
- boundary extension  
- horizon directionality  
- commitment constraints  

Propagation is not motion or dynamics — it is **architectural continuation**.

---

# **7. What are commitments?**

Commitments are **structural obligations** encoded by the primitives.

They determine:

- how motifs extend  
- how boundaries stabilize  
- how horizons orient  
- how networks form  

Commitments are not choices — they are **architectural necessities**.

# **8. What is multiplicity and why does it matter?**

Multiplicity is the coexistence of multiple interacting tendencies.

It is essential because:

- without multiplicity, no contrast  
- without contrast, no sign‑flip  
- without sign‑flip, no structure  

Multiplicity is the **source of richness** in ED architecture.

Motifs connect through:

- shared boundaries  
- aligned horizons  
- reinforcing commitments  

This produces **structural networks** — large‑scale architecture built from local rules.

Networks are the **global expression** of ED structure.
