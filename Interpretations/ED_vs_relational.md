# ED_vs_relational.md
### *How ED differs from relational ontology*

ED is relational — but not in the same way as classical relational ontologies (Barbour, Rovelli, Leibniz, etc.).  
This document clarifies the distinction.

---

## 1. Classical relational ontology

Traditional relational frameworks assert:

- no absolute space  
- no absolute time  
- only relations between entities exist  

But they still assume:

- objects  
- states  
- configurations  
- trajectories  

ED rejects these assumptions.

---

## 2. ED is relational *and* event‑based

ED does not begin with objects that relate.  
It begins with **events** that participate.

Interpretation rule:

> **Relations are not between objects.  
> Relations *constitute* objects.**

This is a deeper form of relationality.

---

## 3. ED is relational *and* geometry‑first

Classical relational theories often rely on:

- configuration spaces  
- relational coordinates  
- symmetry groups  

ED does not.

ED’s relationality is:

- geometric  
- structural  
- motif‑driven  
- commitment‑constrained  

It is not coordinate‑based.

---

## 4. ED is relational *and* compositional

Classical relational theories often treat relations as static.  
ED treats relations as **compositional**:

- gradients compose  
- motifs compose  
- boundaries compose  
- horizons compose  

Relations are **active structural ingredients**, not passive descriptors.

---

## 5. ED is relational *and* regime‑based

Classical relational theories rely on instantaneous states.  
ED uses **regimes**:

- thickened  
- stable  
- motif‑consistent  
- structurally persistent  

Regimes are not states — they are **structural contexts**.

---

## 6. Summary

ED differs from classical relational ontology because:

- it is **event‑based**, not object‑based  
- it is **geometry‑first**, not coordinate‑first  
- it is **compositional**, not descriptive  
- it is **regime‑based**, not state‑based  
- it treats relations as **structural generators**, not as bookkeeping  

ED is relational — but in a **deeper, structural, architectural** sense.
